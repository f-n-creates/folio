My name's Flora and I'm currently a student at RMIT University studying design in digital media. I call Melbourne (Australia) my home and aspire to help people who help make the lives of others a little better.

I mainly work with UI/UX, websites and motion graphics, yet I admire many types of creative work, such as architecture and interior design.

All projects featured on my portfolio are original and received a high distinction (the highest possible letter grade) in an academic setting.

Mockingbird is a Google Chrome extension for desktop Twitter that changes the headings and names of key functions into their literal intentions to mock the way Twitter fights to keep our attention on their site


Completed — October 2020
Type — Chrome extension
Visit — Here

Inspired by Jenny Odell's ideas on the attention economy, the extension exposes the demands of social media where they push users to spend time on the platform, and emotionally invest into the information available on the platform.

Constructed using Javascript with the help of the Google Developer documents, the extension consists of 4 components; the Mockingbird brand, the delay to the changes, the changes made to the Twitter DOM, and the interactions that trigger certain changes.

The brand

The Mockingbird logo, reimagined from Twitter's existing bluebird logo to a mockingbird, represents the extension. On the DOM, Twitter's logo is replaced by the Mockingbird logo when the extension is active, signifying an altered state of the platform and a separate identity, yet remains in sync with the Twitter brand because of the trademark blue.

The delay

When the user activates Mockingbird and logs onto Twitter, it’ll appear normal until the change kicks in. Delaying the change serves an alarm to awaken obsessive Twitter users who are oblivious to the amount of energy they pour into social media. Ironically, it draws attention to the changed labels and invokes a realisation of the platform’s motives where they grab our attention to force users to stay.

The changes & interactivity

The Tweet button on the menu remains the same to tease and remind users of what used to be. But as the button is clicked on and the modal box appears, the placeholder text eventually switches to match the text on the home page, and as the user types, so does the Tweet button. This impact places emphasis on the change as it is visible, and makes the user question themselves before they tweet.

When the user scrolls down, it triggers the wormhole where the feed is what birds tend to eat, but is also a tunnel that sucks you in to endless scrolling. The same thing occurs where the headings on the notifications, bookmarks and lists pages are changed upon scroll.

On the 'messages' page, a chat needs to be clicked on to cause the title change. Similarly to the modal box, this trigger was chosen with user interaction in mind which adds to the goal of raising awareness in Twitter users, and becomes a warning that asks them to think twice before engaging with the platform to eventually deter them from frequent usage and lurking.
